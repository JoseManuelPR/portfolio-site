## The Setup

Working from Chiclayo, Peru for companies in San Francisco means a 2-3 hour time difference. That's manageable, but the real challenges are more nuanced than timezone math.

## Communication is Architecture

In a distributed team, communication patterns shape the product as much as code architecture. A few principles I've adopted:

**Write everything down.** If it's not in a document, Slack thread, or PR description, it doesn't exist. I spend 30% more time writing context than I would in an office, but the payoff in alignment is massive.

**Over-communicate intent, not just output.** Instead of "I refactored the auth module," I write: "I refactored the auth module to support the upcoming SSO feature. This changes how tokens are validated but maintains backward compatibility. Here's why..."

## Async-First, Sync When It Matters

Not everything needs a meeting. My rule of thumb:

- **Async**: status updates, code reviews, design feedback, documentation
- **Sync**: architecture decisions, conflict resolution, brainstorming, onboarding

## The Multilingual Advantage

Speaking Spanish, English, and Portuguese has been unexpectedly powerful. Beyond the obvious communication benefits, it builds empathy for users and teammates who think differently about problems.

## Building Trust Remotely

Trust in remote work is built through consistency, not presence. Show up reliably, deliver what you promise, raise blockers early, and celebrate teammates' wins publicly. After a few months, the timezone gap becomes invisible.

## Advice for Engineers in Latin America

1. Invest in your English — written English matters more than spoken in remote work
2. Build in public — GitHub contributions, blog posts, and open source speak louder than resumes
3. Don't undersell yourself — the quality of engineering in LATAM is world-class
4. Find your overlap hours — protect 3-4 hours of overlap with your team's timezone
